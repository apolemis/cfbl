Where Sports Meets Everything Else That Matters

The Shot: An Oral History of the 2006 Choate Rosemary Hall IBL Playoff Miracle

An xpage.com/cfbl Longform Production

They will tell you it was just an intramural basketball game. They will tell you it was a lucky shot, that the gym was half empty, that nobody who mattered was watching. Those people were not there. Or they were there and they have not yet reckoned with what they saw. Mo Dinkers was a late-round pick with a great name and no particular reputation, and he caught the ball at half court with the clock dying and threw it at the basket the way you throw something when you have nothing to lose, and it went in, and nothing was the same. This is that story.

Part One: The Draft

Drew Stanton (team captain, IBL Draft Class of Winter 2006): I remember it like it was yesterday. We’re at Archie Moore’s, right, so you’ve already got the whole table smelling like buffalo sauce and bad decisions. Someone had knocked over a thing of ranch dressing directly onto the draft sheet. Like, directly. A catastrophic ranch event. I’m squinting at this paper trying to figure out who’s left on the board and half the names are just gone. Illegible. There’s a smear that might be a person. But then, through the grease, clear as day — Mo Dinkers. I don’t know anything about this kid. I don’t know if he can dribble. I don’t know if he’s ever seen a basketball court. All I know is that name. Mo Dinkers. You draft that name. That’s not a choice, that’s a calling.

Will Magioncalda (team captain, IBL Draft Class of Winter 2006): I saw him circle that name. I thought he’d lost his mind. I was trying to read the sheet myself and I couldn’t make out anything except what I believe was a last name that started with a W, and I drafted that instead. Turned out to be a kid who showed up once and said he had a conflict.

Tony Polemis (team captain, IBL Draft Class of Winter 2006): I had my eye on someone else entirely but the dressing had completely destroyed that section of the page. So I just pointed at a dry patch and said that one. That’s how I got Galker Dupotton.

Drew Stanton: Third round. Maybe fourth. Listen, the ranch situation had compromised the round headers too. I circled the name. My life changed.

Mr. Bjorkdahl (IBL coordinator / facilities associate, Choate Rosemary Hall): The draft? Sure, yeah. I put out the sheet. I think I taped it to a clipboard. The league is mostly run by the kids, honestly. I show up, I blow the whistle sometimes. It’s fine. Do I think it’s a historically significant league? I mean. No. But it’s better than my previous janitorial position cleaning the Tuck Shop grill, I’ll tell you that much. You ever tried to get two weeks of accumulated beef fat off a flat-top with a putty knife? In January? I’ll take the basketball.

Dr. Funk (affiliation unclear): You gotta understand the draft from an energetic standpoint. In the streets we called that the “blind pick,” you feel me? When the paperwork is compromised, the universe is making the selection. Ranch dressing ain’t an accident. Ranch dressing is a meridian. Clinically speaking, the body’s decision-making cortex is most active when the conscious mind is, and I say this with full academic authority, absolutely cooked. Drew saw that name and his amygdala said: yes. That’s the science. Also I was in the Hindu Kush at the time and none of this is my business.

Part Two: The Team, Such As It Was

Mr. Bjorkdahl: I want to be clear that the league has a very thorough rulebook. I wrote most of it during a maintenance staff meeting about the new leaf blower policy. It covers fouls, out of bounds, the mercy rule. It’s a serious document. My wife has not read it but I’ve described it to her and she agrees it sounds thorough.

Galker Dupotton (combo guard, Class of 2009): We’re not one person.

Galker Dupotton: I want to be really clear about that. We don’t know each other. We met when they announced the teams. Someone looked at the sheet and said “Galker Dupotton, you’re up” and we both stood up because apparently someone had written our names together as a joke or an error and it just — stuck. The league never corrected it.

Galker Dupotton: We’ve been in the same gym multiple times now and we still barely speak. He does his thing, I do my thing. We have different strengths.

Galker Dupotton: But the stat sheet has us combined. One line. “Galker Dupotton: 14 points, 6 rebounds.” Which of us had the 14 points? We genuinely don’t know. There’s no breakdown.

Galker Dupotton: It’s dehumanizing, honestly.

Giovanni D’Amico (teammate, witnessed the shot from the left baseline): Before I tell you about the shot — and I will tell you, because it deserves to be told properly — I need to explain the atmosphere. The gymnasium, she was electric. You could feel it in your bones. We had come too far, we had fought too hard. This was not just basketball. This was destiny. The shot — when it went up, time stopped. I am not a religious man but I crossed myself. My grandmother, who was not present and did not know this was happening, probably felt something that evening. A warmth. A disturbance. This is what a truly great sporting moment does to the blood of a man.

Mo Dinkers (shooter): Yeah I knew it was going in.

Part Three: The Fuel

Vinny (proprietor, Vinny’s Deli, Wallingford, CT): Basketball? They were playing basketball that afternoon? Three, four of those kids came in maybe an hour before the game. I made them the Mario Lanza. That’s the meatballs with pepperoni, ricotta cheese, and mozzarella, and I put the hot peppers on there because they asked. On a foot-long. Each. I’m not a doctor. But I will say, I was surprised to learn there was athletic activity occurring so close to the Mario Lanza. That sandwich was designed for watching something. A movie. A Sunday. Not running. They played basketball after that? God bless them.

Will Magioncalda: Here’s the thing people don’t understand about that season. The year before, the IBL was a completely different game. Fast. Up and down, push it in transition, first team to fifty wins. Beautiful basketball, honestly. Pure.

Tony Polemis: We found Vinny’s sometime in October and that was it. The entire strategic identity of the league changed overnight. You can’t run after a Mario Lanza. Physiologically, it is not possible.

Will Magioncalda: You had to adapt. You couldn’t push the break anymore because half the guys were dealing with cramping by the second half. We’re talking genuine cramping. Like, stop-the-game cramping.

Tony Polemis: So the game slowed down. It had to. Everything moved to the half court. Sets, deliberate offense, you’re working the ball around, waiting for your body to reach some kind of equilibrium with the sandwich. In a weird way, Vinny’s made us all better half-court players.

Will Magioncalda: Which is the only reason Mo Dinkers’ shot even made sense as a shot. A year earlier, nobody takes that. You push it up the floor, you get a layup, game over. But this version of the IBL? The half court heave was a legitimate weapon. Vinny built that.

Vinny: I appreciate that but I want to be clear I take no responsibility for anything that happened in that gymnasium.

Dr. Funk: I’ve seen this before. Clinically. High calorie intake prior to explosive athletic performance — there’s a school of thought, very underground, very respected in certain zip codes, that says the body stores that energy like a capacitor. You’re not full, you’re loaded. Charged. In med school we called that “sandwich plasma.” I may have coined that term. I was going through a lot at the time.

Part Four: The Shot

Mr. Bjorkdahl: The clock was running out. I know that because I was the one watching the clock. I had a stopwatch that belonged to the PE department. It has a little crack on it from an unrelated incident I’d rather not get into.

Mike Morrill (game announcer, IBL Playoff, Winter 2006): I’d never announced anything before that day. Someone handed me a microphone and said go. I want to be honest — I had been thinking seriously about media. Broadcasting, journalism, that whole world. I thought I had something to offer.

Giovanni D’Amico: The ball came to Mo and I wanted to — I was open, you know? I was open on the wing. But Mo, he sees the court differently than other men. Or he doesn’t see it at all. With Mo it’s hard to know. And then he looks up, he sees the clock, and he releases the ball from half court like he is throwing it to God himself. The arc on this ball.

Adit Sreevasan (point guard, uninvolved team): I was there. I want to be clear about that. I was in the gym for the whole game. I arrived at approximately 3:45 in the afternoon, which you can verify, and I did not leave until the final buzzer. I was standing near the water fountain on the south wall. Several people saw me.

George “Gator” Gibson (Vagrant and part-time Frenchman street vocalist): So they pass me the ball right? And I say, “wait…wait…wait…no YOU shoot it Mo!”

[Security camera footage reviewed by xPage.com/cfbl confirms Gibson was in minimum security prison in Louisiana at the time of The Shot.]

Mike Morrill: When that ball left his hand I just started screaming. I don’t remember what I said.

Dr. Funk: The arc was, medically speaking, extraordinary. The hang time suggested an elevation not achievable through conventional physics. I have a colleague in Zurich who has been looking into this. We don’t talk about it openly.

Galker Dupotton: We both saw it. We were standing right there. Together. Separately.

Giovanni D’Amico: When the ball went through — no rim, nothing, just net — I fell to my knees. I am not exaggerating this for effect. I literally fell to my knees. My pants got a mark on them from the gym floor and I never fully got it out. It’s still on those pants. I keep the pants.

Mike Morrill: Here’s the thing. After the buzzer, after the chaos settled down, I’m standing there with the microphone and I’m thinking — what am I chasing? What does a career in media get me that I don’t already have right now, in this gymnasium, at this moment? I had just called the greatest shot in basketball history, arguably. I was twenty seconds removed from the peak of what that profession could ever offer me. It only goes down from here. So I put the mic down and I never looked back. I’m in law now. It made complete sense. You can’t top it. You just can’t.

Noel Titus (small forward, Class of 2009): There are caves in France where men painted animals on the walls forty thousand years ago and scholars argue about what it meant and whether it was sacred and what they were trying to say to the darkness. I do not wonder about this. I know what they were doing. They were trying to hold onto something that happened that they knew would not happen again. I understand the impulse. I was there in that gym.

Mr. Bjorkdahl: Yeah, the ball went in. It was a good shot. I blew the whistle. The kids seemed excited. I had to cone off part of the floor afterward because of the celebration, a little juice got spilled. I coned it off. That’s what I’m there for.

Part Five: The Aftermath

Jordan Abrams (teammate, three-point specialist): I was calling for the ball. I was wide open. Everyone in that gym knows I was open. Mo is a ball hog. I’ve been saying this since the day the season started. Put me in that situation, I make that shot nine times out of ten. I have range. My range is documented.

[At this point, the interviewer produced a statistical sheet showing Abrams shooting 3-for-46 from three-point range over the course of the IBL season, a clip of approximately 6.5 percent.]

Jordan Abrams: I don’t — where did you get this? These numbers are wrong. I don’t know who compiled this but I don’t trust the methodology.

[Abrams stood up and left the room. He did not return.]

Mo Dinkers: That shot was the beginning of everything for me. And I mean everything. The exposure alone — look, there are brands out there, real brands, who are going to want a piece of what I built in that gym. Half-court shots don’t lie. Half-court shots are a resume. You think the people at the top don’t know about this shot? They know. I’ve been approached. I can’t say by who. But I’ve been approached. I’ll tell you this much: I’m not going to spend my life managing other people’s money or doing whatever. I have a shot to market. I have a brand. Mo Dinkers is not a regular name and it’s not going to have a regular life.

Drew Stanton: When I drafted him, I just liked the name. I had no idea. But now I look back and I think — what if the ranch dressing hadn’t spilled? What if the names were legible? Maybe I draft someone else. Maybe Mo Dinkers doesn’t get his shot. Maybe this whole thing doesn’t happen. Ranch dressing changed my life. I’m at peace with that.

Tony Polemis: People want to give Mo the credit, and fine, give Mo the credit. But the real story is Vinny’s. The game slowed down, the half court opened up, and a kid with a good name became a legend. You follow that chain back and it ends with a loaf of bread, packed with meatballs and pepperoni. That’s the origin story.

Will Magioncalda: I think about the previous season sometimes. The fast break season. If that version of the IBL is still the IBL in 2006, that shot never happens. Mo Dinkers is just a man. But Vinny’s changed the game. Literally changed it. And the game gave us the shot. So yeah. I think about that.

Adit Sreevasan: I just want to reiterate, for the record, that I was present in that gymnasium and only that gymnasium on that date and time. I didn’t leave campus that week. Or that month. I have receipts from Vinny’s. I bought a Snapple at 2pm. The timestamp is right there. That is the kind of person I am. A person who buys Snapple and watches basketball and has no international footprint whatsoever.

Dr. Funk: In the end, the shot is just a vessel. The real story is about belief. About a young man, a smudged piece of paper, a catastrophic amount of meatballs consumed in the hours before, and a ball that defied what we know about reality. I wrote a paper on it. The journal wouldn’t publish it. Their loss. Their tremendous loss.

Mr. Bjorkdahl: Did they advance to the finals? Yes. Did they win the finals? I want to say yes. I have the bracket somewhere. Look, the point is the season concluded and I didn’t have to clean the grill for six weeks and that was a genuinely wonderful period of my life.

Other Adit (Choate Rosemary Hall, Class of 2008): I wasn’t even in IBL. I think you have the wrong Adit.

Giovanni D’Amico: The shot lives in me. Every day. When I close my eyes. When I am eating. When I am in the shower and I am thinking about my life and what it means and where I am going — the ball is still in the air. It will always be in the air. What a thing to have witnessed. What a thing.

Mo Dinkers could not be reached for final comment because, according to his representative, he was “in discussions.” The discussions were not elaborated upon. Jordan Abrams has declined all further interview requests. Galker Dupotton remain, per IBL records, one person. Mr. Bjorkdahl was recently appointed co-chair of the leaf blower subcommittee, in addition to his IBL duties. He seems genuinely content. Adit Sreevasan provided the editorial team with seventeen pages of supporting documentation confirming his whereabouts on the day of the shot. We did not ask for this. Other Adit could not be reached for comment, which is probably fine.

DAN RUDEL × TONY POLEMIS: SAXOPHONE, BASKETBALL & THE HUMAN CONDITION

📜 ED. NOTE FROM THE XPAGE CFBL DESK

Welcome back to the Page 3, xpage.com/cfbl’s flagship interview series where we smash two worlds together like a Shaquille O’Neal dunk on a 9-foot rim. This week we’ve got saxophone virtuoso DAN RUDEL sitting down with intramural basketball legend TONY POLEMIS for a no-holds-barred conversation about music, the hardwood, and the human condition. Neither of these men needed to do this interview. Both of them agreed immediately. We provided sandwiches. What follows is lightly edited for clarity and heavily unedited for chaos.

TONY: Dan. Big fan. Huge. I want to start by asking — what is the saxophone, in basketball terms. Like just explain it to me.

DAN: Okay. The saxophone is the point guard of the woodwind family. It sees the whole floor. It can score, it can facilitate. The clarinet? That’s your role player. Solid. Does its job. Nobody’s putting the clarinet on a poster.

TONY: I respect that. I respect the hierarchy. Because in basketball, same thing — there’s a floor general and there’s guys who just kind of stand in the corner hoping something happens to them. I have guys like that on my squad. Jordan Abram. Jordan is a triangle in a saxophone band.

DAN: Jordan sounds like an oboe to me, actually.

TONY: Jordan IS an oboe. That’s the most accurate thing anyone has ever said about Jordan. Okay my turn — when you’re playing a jazz solo, is that like when I’m taking the ball coast to coast in the open floor? Because I feel like I’m improvising out there. Reading the defense. Creating.

DAN: Honestly? Yes. A jazz solo is absolutely the coast-to-coast drive. You have a key, which is like your lane, but within that you’re making micro-decisions every fraction of a second. The difference is in jazz if you make a mistake we call it a “blue note” and pretend it was intentional.

TONY: We call that a “shot fake” in basketball.

DAN: Exactly the same thing.

TONY: Exactly. So here’s what I’ve been thinking. The pick-and-roll — and stay with me here — is basically counterpoint, right? Two voices, two players, moving in opposition to create something neither could create alone.

DAN: That’s… actually a decent analogy.

TONY: I’ve been workshopping it.

DAN: I’ll push back slightly. Counterpoint is more like a pick-and-roll where both players are equally important and the play only works if they’ve internalized the same harmonic logic over years of practice. Whereas a pick-and-roll can be run effectively by two strangers who just met.

TONY: Yeah that’s my team. We run it though. Does it work? I’d say forty percent of the time it works every time.

DAN: That’s statistically a problem.

TONY: My team is statistically a problem, Dan. We are 4 and 11.

DAN: Can I ask you something? When you’re in a close game, fourth quarter, down two — what’s going through your head?

TONY: Nothing. Genuinely nothing. And I think that’s the zone. Athletes call it the zone. Is there a zone in music?

DAN: Absolutely. It’s called flow state. You stop thinking about the fingering, stop thinking about the changes, and you’re just inside the music reacting. Some nights I’ll finish a set and I genuinely can’t tell you what I played.

TONY: See that happens to me every game but I thought it was because of the acid reflux caused by all those Vinny’s sandwiches.

DAN: It might be both.

TONY: Okay I want to talk about the reed situation because I looked this up. You bite on a piece of wood?

DAN: It’s not wood, it’s cane. And you don’t bite it, you support it with your embouchure — the shape of your mouth and lips — and it vibrates to produce the sound.

TONY: So your mouth IS the pick-and-roll.

DAN: …My mouth is the pick-and-roll.

TONY: Your lips set the screen and your breath is the ball handler.

DAN: I’ve never thought about it that way and I went to conservatory for four years.

TONY: I never went to conservatory but I do have a podcast.

DAN: Those are equivalent credentials honestly.

TONY: Here’s my big question for you. Do you have a signature move? Because I have a signature move. I back those boys down in the paint and hit them with the jump hook.

DAN: I have a move where I start a phrase like I’m going somewhere completely predictable and then at the last possible moment I take it somewhere nobody expected. The listeners lean in. They think they know where I’m going.

TONY: And then?

DAN: I hit them with the jazz hook.

TONY: That is the exact same move. We have the same move.

DAN: I think we might be the same person.

TONY: I think we are. We’re just — one of us is doing it with a ball and one is doing it with a horn and society has decided those are different things but I’m not sure society is right.

DAN: Society is frequently wrong.

TONY: Deeply wrong. Okay. I want to get into something more personal because I think this is where the real interview is.

DAN: Let’s go.

TONY: Size.

DAN: Size.

TONY: I am a large man. You are a compact man.

DAN: I prefer “compact.” That’s very diplomatic of you.

TONY: I’m a diplomat. I’m 6’4”, 245. I bump my head on things constantly. Like, every day. It’s just a thing that happens to me. I walked into a ceiling fan last Tuesday.

DAN: A ceiling fan.

TONY: It was on. It was spinning. Full contact. Just as a regular part of my Tuesday.

DAN: I cannot ride most roller coasters.

TONY: What?

DAN: Height minimums. I’m an adult man who gets turned away at the ride.

TONY: That is genuinely harrowing. I’m so sorry.

DAN: The teenager with the measuring stick just looks at me and I already know. I know before I even get to the front of the line. I’ve known since the parking lot.

TONY: See for me it’s the opposite — I can ride the roller coaster but I cannot get INTO the roller coaster. The restraint bar situation. My knees are against the seat in front and everyone in my row hates me before we’ve even crested the first hill.

DAN: We’re both being failed by the roller coaster industrial complex from opposite ends.

TONY: And who is the roller coaster designed for?

DAN: Medium guys.

TONY: Medium guys. Always. Airplane seats — medium guys. They fit. I’m folded like origami in coach. You’re sitting in there and your feet are dangling.

DAN: My feet have never touched the floor of an airplane. Not once. I fly like a child.

TONY: People ask me to reach things for them constantly. Strangers. In grocery stores. Like I’m a public service.

DAN: People ask me if I need help reaching things and they say it with a face.

TONY: The face.

DAN: The face, yes.

TONY: I know the face from the other side. I’ve made the face. I owe you an apology on behalf of tall people.

DAN: Accepted. In return I apologize for every time a short person has walked directly under your eyeline at a concert and you couldn’t see anything.

TONY: Thank you. That means a lot.

DAN: Also doorways. You’re constantly doing the duck-and-lean.

TONY: Older buildings are trying to kill me. Colonial-era doorframes were designed for people who were five foot six and dying of something. I have to do a full theatrical bow every time I enter a tavern.

DAN: I can stand up straight in every tavern.

TONY: Must be nice.

DAN: It’s the only structural advantage I have and I will not apologize for enjoying it.

TONY: You know what though — the medium guys, they just glide through the world. No friction. The seat fits. The doorframe clears. They reach the grocery store shelf without drama. They board the roller coaster and the bar clicks into place like God intended.

DAN: They don’t think about space. They just exist in it.

TONY: Oblivious. Comfortable. Sleeping fine.

DAN: It must be incredible.

TONY: It sounds boring, honestly.

DAN: That’s a generous way to look at it.

TONY: Look — I think what you and I have figured out today is that adversity is the whole game. Whether it’s the ceiling fan or the roller coaster or the Tuesday night squad giving up 67 points to accountants, it’s the friction that makes it interesting.

DAN: That’s what jazz is. Jazz is the ceiling fan.

TONY: Basketball is the measuring stick.

DAN: We’re the same.

TONY: We’re the same, Dan. We’re exactly the same.

DAN: Different sizes.

TONY: Tragically different sizes. But the same.

Both men agreed this was “the best interview they’d ever done” within thirty seconds of it ending. xpage.com/cfbl takes no responsibility for the ceiling fan.
